Why I Started My PhD with Customer Discovery

Add a cover image here

Most PhD projects start with a literature review. Mine started with a question: "What do coaches and physiotherapists actually need?"

Conventional PhD Path

The traditional PhD follows a comfortable, linear path: find a gap in the literature, fill it, publish, move on. The problem is that a gap in the literature isn't always a gap in practice. You can spend four years answering a question nobody is asking.

As I started learning about entrepreneurship, I realized this is the exact same mistake that kills startups. Teams build something because it's technically interesting, not because it provides value to the user. In research, we do the same thing: we optimize for novelty and publication, not for whether anyone will use what we develop.

What I Did Differently

Before my PhD, I was a Strength and Conditioning Coach. I'd been the end user. I knew firsthand that science rarely drove decision-making in practice, even at a time when "data-informed training" was becoming the buzzword of the industry. The gap between what researchers published and what coaches actually used was enormous.

During my masters, I built a machine learning framework to profile sprint technique. It worked. But I knew it would never see the inside of a training facility. The data collection was too cumbersome to run during a team session, and one-on-one assessments with the technology would have been prohibitively expensive. I'd built a good tool that solved the wrong deployment problem.

That realization pushed me toward entrepreneurship. I enrolled in an incubator program where I started learning about customer discovery, product-market fit, and the parallels between the scientific method and startup process. It reframed how I thought about research entirely: don't just ask "is this novel?" -- instead ask "is this useful, accessible, and scalable?"

So, during the first semester of my PhD, I ran a structured discovery process. Through a program called Lab2Market Validate, I conducted 41 interviews with strength and conditioning coaches, physiotherapists, and industry experts. I wasn't pitching a product or looking to provide them answers. I wanted to learn from them. I wanted to know what their current process was, where it was broken, and if they thought it was broken.

Three patterns emerged clearly: (1) existing technology was too expensive, (2) it didn't scale to team environments without extensive hardware and personnel costs, (3) it didn't deliver insights people could act on. These weren't surprises to me; I'd lived every one of them on the practitioner side. But hearing them repeated across 41 conversations turned my personal frustrations into a validated problem. That problem became my PhD.

What Changed After I Ran My Discovery

Before I did this, I wrote a grant proposal for my PhD funding. The equipment budget alone was $100,000 CAD. Gold-standard motion capture systems, force plates, the full lab setup. When I investigated the scientific literature, there was genuine value in the work. The science was sound.

But here's what the conversations revealed: there was already a mountain of biomechanical knowledge sitting in journals that would never reach a single coach or clinician. Not because the research was bad, but because it was locked behind equipment that costs six figures, requires a dedicated lab space, and needs a trained technician to operate. The knowledge existed. The delivery mechanism was broken.

So, I threw that proposal in the garbage.

I pivoted my entire PhD. The new direction didn't start with "what can I study?" -- it started with "what's preventing this knowledge from actually being used?" The answer was accessibility. The technology constraint was the problem, not the science.

My new project became focused on building an AI pipeline that could accurately capture someone's movement from a single smartphone video and translate it into actionable insights -- the kind that help a practitioner reduce someone's pain or improve their performance. No lab. No $100k setup. Just a phone and an algorithm.

That pivot, from studying movement in a lab to making movement analysis work everywhere, came directly from listening to the people who would use the output.

Turning Discovery into Technical Specs

It was clear to me that if this project was going to land it needed to:

1. Minimal Hardware Costs: Most existing setups used multi-camera systems costing tens of thousands of dollars. We needed to deliver lab-grade data from a single camera. If the barrier to adoption is equipment, remove the equipment, and use something virtually everyone on this earth owns.

2. An architecture built for deployment, not performance: The model needed to balance accuracy with real-world constraints: offline processing, no lab infrastructure, and fast enough to be useful in practical workflow. We weren't optimizing for benchmark scores; we were optimizing for a coach being able to use it during a session.

3. Movement Quality Scoring that didn't require a PhD to interpret: The output couldn't be joint angle time series that only a biomechanist could read. We needed metrics that were scientifically rigorous but clear enough that a practitioner could look at them and make a decision.

Every decision we made was around satisfying these three constraints. Minimal hardware costs, model architectures that produced "good enough" data in deployment conditions, and scoring that could be interpreted and actioned by someone who isn't a researcher.

Validating the Direction -- With Money, Not Just Citations

When you choose a PhD direction like this: translational, applied, going against the grain, funding gets a little complicated. In Canada, most research funding is structured around basic science. There aren't many portals designed for work that sits between a lab and a product. You're an outlier, and the system isn't built for outliers.

But the customer discovery work opened doors that a traditional proposal wouldn't have.

The initial work was funded through an internal grant at Brock University focused on research commercialization. That proof of concept, built on the back of the discovery research, became the foundation for a two-year Mitacs Accelerate Entrepreneurship grant worth $180K. The grant wasn't awarded for the science alone. It was awarded because the research had a clear commercial pathway. The discovery conversation was the evidence this wasn't just a good idea; it was a needed one.

The funding gave me a lifeline that allowed me to devote my energy full time into taking on this massive PhD project.

Why More Researchers Should Do This

As PhD students, we're constantly told our work needs to have impact. The bizarre reality is that when we only translate our knowledge through journal articles, the "impact" amounts to a couple dozen citations in papers that are more likely to be used as training data for large language models than read by the people our research was meant to help. Impact factor doesn't measure actual impact.

To have real impact you have to leave the lab and enter the real world. The good news is that science and entrepreneurship go hand in hand. During my latest incubator experience with Lab2Market Launch, my mentor Daryl Sherman put it simply: "Businesses are built on data, not feelings or ideas." As scientists we already have world-class skills designing experiments, developing reliable measures, and interpreting data. Those are exactly the skills entrepreneurship demands: run small experiments, measure the outcomes, decide where to go next.

Customer discovery taught me that the best research doesn't just answer a question. It solves a problem someone is willing to act on. That mindset changed my PhD. It's how I think about every problem now.
