Multijoint Coordination Contributes to the Minimization of Frontal Plane Center-of-Mass Displacement in Maximal Velocity Sprinting

Vellucci, C.L., Beaudette, S.

Journal of Applied Biomechanics, 41(6), 561–570 · 2024

Abstract

Most sprint research focuses on what faster sprinters do differently, like longer strides or shorter ground contact times. But very little work has looked at how sprinters stabilize themselves while running at top speed, specifically how much their body sways side to side during each step. We found that the amount of lateral sway in a sprinter's center of mass during the stance phase was significantly linked to how fast they could sprint. Less side to side displacement meant faster velocities. That part alone is useful, but we also wanted to understand how faster sprinters keep that sway in check. It turns out that specific coordination patterns between the limbs and trunk play a big role. The way the knees flex and extend together, along with how the ribcage and pelvis rotate relative to each other, were the most important factors in keeping lateral displacement to a minimum. What makes this exciting from a practical standpoint is that lateral sway is something you can actually measure in the field with wearable sensors. It gives coaches and practitioners a simple, interpretable number to assess sprint technique quality without needing a full lab setup. It also opens the door to designing more targeted coaching cues and rehab strategies around postural control during sprinting.
